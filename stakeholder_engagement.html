Who is involved

Key Stakeholders

The energy community is open and voluntary — anyone in the village can join. Six categories of stakeholder are essential for the system to function, each contributing different inputs (waste, land, governance, expertise) and receiving different benefits (energy, fertiliser, income, legitimacy).

🏨
Primary waste provider · Summer

Hotels & Airbnbs

The largest source of food waste during peak season. Directly benefit from cheaper cooking gas and hot water (Phase 1) and lower electricity bills (Phase 2). Their participation is the economic engine of the summer feedstock cycle.

Their motivation: Reduced LPG and electricity costs; sustainability credentials for eco-conscious tourists; future EU waste regulation compliance.

Waste inputEnergy offtaker
🍽️
Supplementary provider · Summer

Tavernas & Cafés

Smaller but numerous, tavernas extend the hotel feedstock window into the shoulder season. Many are family-run and deeply embedded in the community, making them natural early adopters who carry social influence.

Their motivation: Waste disposal cost savings; community identity as sustainable businesses; reduced LPG bills.

Waste inputEnergy offtaker
🏡
Year-round member · Core community

Resident Households

The backbone of the energy community. Resident households provide year-round organic waste, stabilising off-season feedstock, and are the primary electricity beneficiaries under virtual net metering. They provide the 30-member threshold required under Law 5037/2023.

Their motivation: Lower electricity bills year-round; local ownership and pride; protection from diesel price volatility.

Waste inputEnergy offtaker
🫒
Off-season feedstock · Digestate recipient

Olive Farmers & Cooperatives

Lesbos's 11 million olive trees generate massive olive mill wastewater (OMW) volumes in November–December — a premium off-season feedstock. Farmers receive digestate as organic fertiliser in return, directly supporting the viability of small-scale cultivation.

Their motivation: Free fertiliser input; OMW disposal solution; participating in the island's circular food economy.

OMW feedstockDigestate recipient
🐟
Supplementary feedstock · Shoulder season

Fishing Cooperative

The Kalloni sardine fishery generates fish-processing by-products year-round. Fish waste has a very high biogas yield and is a valuable supplement in shoulder months. The cooperative brings organisational capacity as a founding member.

Their motivation: Managed disposal of fish processing waste; participation in a community institution that supports local livelihoods.

Fish waste input
🐑
Winter feedstock provider

Livestock Farmers

Sheep and goat farming is widespread on Lesbos. Animal manure is a biologically stable feedstock that regulates digester chemistry in winter, blending well with higher-energy inputs. Farmers also receive digestate in return.

Their motivation: Managed manure disposal; free fertiliser; community membership and cheaper local energy.

Manure inputDigestate recipient
🏛️
Enabler & anchor member

Municipality of Lesbos

Provides land for the digester site, integrates with municipal waste collection, and co-governs the energy community as an institutional anchor member — following the Minoan EnCom model where three municipalities joined. Adds long-term stability and legitimacy.

Their motivation: Municipal waste obligations; EU Green Deal alignment; local economic development.

Grid regulator · Phase 2

DEDDIE / HEDNO

The Distribution Network Operator responsible for grid connection agreements and virtual net metering approvals in Phase 2. Not a member but a critical external enabler. Early engagement is essential — grid capacity must be confirmed before CHP installation.

Their motivation: Statutory obligation to support renewable energy communities; reducing peak diesel generation costs on island grids.

Research & knowledge partner

University of the Aegean

Monitors, measures, and publishes pilot results — providing the academic credibility that supports funding applications and replication. The university's presence on Lesbos is a direct asset: students and researchers can be embedded in the operation from day one.

Their motivation: Real-world research site; publications; student fieldwork; island sustainability contribution.

Technical installation & maintenance

Greek Biogas Operator

A domestic technical partner (e.g. Tetoros Machinery, WELTEC's Greek partner with 36+ installations) is essential for digester design, installation, commissioning, and maintenance. No local biogas expertise exists on Lesbos — this relationship is critical for Phase 1 success.

Their motivation: Commercial contract; first island biogas reference project in Greece; Aegean replication market.

InstallationMaintenance
Bottom-up principle: Every stakeholder joins voluntarily. The legal structure under Greek Law 5037/2023 guarantees open membership, democratic governance (one member, one vote), and a primary purpose of community benefit rather than financial profit — distinguishing this from a private biogas company.

Internal governance

Four Working Groups

Modelled directly on the Minoan Energy Community in Crete — which used four working groups to scale from 38 to 200+ members within its first year — the Lesbos community should establish these four functions from the outset. Each group distributes the governance load so no single person carries the whole project.

01

Technical

Oversees all physical aspects of the biogas system.

  • Waste collection scheduling and routes
  • Digester monitoring: pH, temperature, loading rate
  • Gas distribution network maintenance
  • Liaison with Greek biogas technical partner
  • DEDDIE/HEDNO grid connection (Phase 2)
  • Digestate quality testing and farm distribution
02

Administrative

Manages legal compliance, finances, and membership.

  • Energy community registration and legal upkeep
  • Member records and share management
  • Financial accounts and budget oversight
  • EU grant applications (Recovery Plan, Cohesion Funds)
  • Municipal and DEDDIE/HEDNO correspondence
  • Meeting organisation and minutes
03

Promotion & Communication

Grows the membership base and communicates results.

  • Recruitment of new households, hotels, and farms
  • Village information sessions and open days
  • Multilingual materials (Greek and English)
  • Social media and local press coverage
  • ArcGIS Story Map updates for external audiences
  • Connections to other Greek energy communities
04

Educational

Builds long-term capacity and community trust.

  • Source separation training for hotels, tavernas, households
  • School programmes and youth engagement
  • Workshops on energy bills and net metering
  • University of the Aegean research dissemination
  • Knowledge exchange visits to Minoan EnCom (Crete)

How to engage the community

Stakeholder Engagement Strategy

Different stakeholders need to be engaged differently. The engagement ladder below maps each stage from initial awareness through to full co-governance — the journey every member takes from hearing about the project to running it.

Level 1Inform

Make the community aware the project exists. Passive communication — no commitment required.

  • Village meeting in the square — open to all, informal, in Greek
  • One-page leaflet (Greek and English) to every household and hotel
  • ArcGIS Story Map shared via local Facebook groups and municipal notice boards
  • Short explainer video using local footage, narrated in Greek
Level 2Consult

Gather feedback, surface concerns, and adapt the design based on local knowledge. Essential before finalising the pilot site or governance structure.

  • Structured interviews with local informants
  • Focus group with hotel owners: waste, energy costs, trust in collective schemes
  • Focus group with farmers: fertiliser needs, OMW disposal, land availability
  • Household survey: current energy bills, awareness of energy communities, willingness to join
Level 3Involve

Active participation in shaping the project. Stakeholders move from audience to contributor.

  • Co-design workshops: governance rules, benefit-sharing structure
  • Site visits to functioning Greek energy communities (Minoan EnCom in Crete)
  • Founding member recruitment: first 30 households, hotels, and farms who commit to join
  • Working group formation: invite specific individuals to lead each group
Level 4Collaborate

Shared decision-making. Stakeholders have formal roles in running the cooperative.

  • General Assembly: annual meeting of all members, one vote each regardless of share size
  • Board of Directors: elected from members, oversees all four working groups
  • Working group membership: open to any member — hotel owner, farmer, or household
  • Transparent financial reporting: annual accounts shared with all members
Level 5Empower

The community runs the system independently. The founding team steps back from operational roles.

  • Community members trained to operate and troubleshoot the digester
  • Members vote on Phase 2 (CHP, electricity) investment decisions
  • Peer-to-peer recruitment: existing members invite neighbours and businesses
  • Knowledge export: Lesbos community shares learnings with other Aegean island communities

Reference case study

Minoan Energy Community, Crete

The closest and most instructive model for the Lesbos biogas energy community is the Minoan Energy Community in Crete — a Greek island energy cooperative that demonstrates exactly the kind of rapid, bottom-up, multi-stakeholder growth the Lesbos project aims to replicate.

Source: CONGREGATE Report, INZEB / European Climate Initiative, April 2021 — p. 66

Minoan Energy Community, Crete — Founded October 2019

Based on the island of Crete, Minoan EnCom was founded in October 2019 with 38 members. Within a year of operation, the community built a membership base exceeding 200 — including farmers, urban citizens, local cooperatives, three municipalities, and the Regional Authority of Crete.

Four working groups were formed from the outset — Technical, Administrative, Promotion & Communication, and Educational — exactly the structure proposed for the Lesbos community. Minoan EnCom is currently planning a wide range of renewable energy projects across the island.

Future expansion

How the Project Grows

The pilot is the proof of concept. Once operational, the model replicates — first to other Lesbos villages, then across the non-interconnected Aegean islands.

Phase 1 — Year 1–2

Pilot village — thermal energy

One village. 4–6 hotels and 30 households as founding members. Biogas piped directly to kitchens and water heaters. No grid connection required. University of the Aegean monitors and publishes results.

Phase 2 — Year 3–4

Electricity generation + replication

CHP unit added; grid connection with DEDDIE/HEDNO; electricity shared via virtual net metering. A second village on Lesbos adopts the same model using the pilot's governance template and technical documentation.

Phase 3 — Year 5+

Aegean island network

Multiple communities operating across Lesbos. The model shared with Chios, Samos, and other non-interconnected islands. Lesbos becomes a reference site for biogas energy communities in rural Mediterranean tourism destinations.
